## I. Introduction
AI has been making its way into pretty much every field, and it only makes sense that it would be in software engineering. Writing code, fixing bugs, or just explaining why something isn't working, AI has become a normal part of how a lot of developers work today. As a student in ICS 314 at UH Manoa, I got to see firsthand how these tools fit into the process of actually learning software engineering. Out of all the AI tools available, I only used Claude throughout this course. I didn't use ChatGPT, GitHub Copilot, or Bard. 

## II. Personal Experience with AI
1. Experience WODs (e.g., E18)

I did all of the experience WODs on my own. If I got stuck, I went to the walkthrough videos the professor provided before I asked AI. Honestly, the videos were good enough that I rarely needed anything else. These felt like the right place to actually wrestle with the material since they were low stakes, and I didn't think it was worth it to shortcut.

2. In-class Practice WODs

I worked through these independently, and if there was leftover work after the session I kept trying on my own before going anywhere else for help. I only turned to AI if I was completely stuck and had already tried everything I could think of.

3. In-class WODs

For the graded WODs, I really wanted to get through them on my own as much as possible. The exception was when I ran into a bug and the clock was ticking. In that case, spending several minutes digging through documentation wasn't realistic, so a quick AI question made more sense than burning time. Something like, “My output for this section doesn’t match the expected, what could I be doing wrong?” But even then it was very targeted, not asking Claude to just solve the whole thing for me.

4. Essays

For essays, I mostly used Grammarly to check grammar and catch any obvious mistakes. I didn't use Claude for writing them because the whole point of those assignments was to reflect on my own experience. 

5. Final Project

This is where I used AI the most, and honestly more than I expected. The HTML and CSS side of things was fine, but once we got into the backend stuff it got a lot more complicated. I used Claude to get guidance on how to structure things and understand how different parts of the app were supposed to work together. Something like asking "I'm trying to connect my database to a React component through an API route, how should I set that up?" was the kind of question I was asking. Claude would give me a starting point and then I'd have to figure out how to actually apply it to our specific project.

6. Learning a Concept / Tutorial

When something wasn't clicking, I'd ask Claude to explain it to me using specific questions about the parts I wasn’t sure of. Being able to ask a direct question and get an explanation that was actually tailored to what I was confused about was a lot more useful than re-reading the same paragraph. I could follow up with more questions or ask for a simple example, which made it feel more like a conversation than just looking stuff up.

7. Answering a Question in Class or in Discord

I never used AI for this. If I answered a question in class or on Discord, it was because I knew the answer from my own experience. I only jumped in if I thought I already knew the answer, as it wouldn’t make sense to ask AI to answer someone’s question; if they wanted to ask AI, they wouldn’t have put it in our Discord. 

8. Asking or Answering a Smart-Question

Same thing here. I didn't use AI when working on smart-questions. The point of AI, to me, is learning—What does answering a Smart-Question have to do with that? Again, the person asking could have asked AI if they wanted to. 

9. Coding Example (e.g., "Give an example of using Underscore .pluck")

I never used AI for this. When I needed to understand how something worked, I looked at the documentation and tried it out myself. I find it easier to understand if I can tweak something myself. 

10. Explaining Code

This was one of the more useful things I did with Claude, especially during the final project. Working in a team meant I was reading other people's code pretty regularly, and I would sometimes run into something and can't immediately tell what it's doing. I would still read it, but sometimes I’d run into snags, and I'd paste it into Claude and ask for a plain English explanation. It was quick, it was accurate for the most part, and it let me get up to speed without slowing myself down. 

11. Writing Code

I used Claude a fair amount here, mostly when I was stuck on a bug I couldn't figure out. I'd describe what was happening or paste in the relevant code and ask what might be going wrong. This came up the most during the final project when things were more complex and bugs were harder to trace. I tried to make sure I actually understood the fix rather than just copy-pasting it, otherwise I'd just run into the same problem again later without knowing why.

12. Documenting Code

I never used AI for documentation. I felt like AI would take longer than me doing it manually. 

13. Quality Assurance

This was probably my most consistent use of Claude throughout the whole course. When I got ESLint errors or something was behaving unexpectedly, pasting the code and asking what was wrong was fast and usually pretty accurate. It saved a lot of time compared to manually hunting through the code trying to figure out what was triggering a lint rule. 

14. Other Uses in ICS 314

Nothing else really came up that wasn't already covered.

## III. Impact on Learning and Understanding
Because I tried to work things out on my own before going to AI, it felt more like a support tool than a replacement. By the time I asked Claude something, I already had enough context from struggling with it myself that the response actually made sense to me. The biggest benefits were in concept explanations and debugging. Having something available to clarify a confusing topic or help me get past a stubborn bug kept things from burning time. The times I didn't use it, like for the WODs and essays and documentation, I think actually made me better in the long run. There's a kind of confidence that comes from solving something yourself that you just don't get from watching someone else solve it, even if that someone is an AI.

## IV. Practical Applications
Outside of ICS 314 my AI use has stayed pretty similar to how I used it in the course. I'll bring it in when I'm stuck or need something explained, but I'm not using it as a primary tool. In personal projects it's been useful for the same things: debugging, clarifying concepts, understanding unfamiliar code.

I didn't take part in the HACC so I can't speak to that directly. That said, I can see how the calculation around using AI would be different in a hackathon. When you have a weekend to build something and the goal is a working product, using every tool available makes a lot more sense than it does in a class where the point is actually learning the material.

## V. Challenges and Opportunities
The hardest part was figuring out when to stop trying on my own and just ask for help. Ask too early and I miss out on the part that actually teaches you something; wait too long and I’ve wasted time that could have gone somewhere useful. I didn’t always get this answer right… 

The other thing I had to learn was not to just trust whatever Claude said. Sometimes the response was slightly off in a way that wasn't immediately obvious, so I got in the habit of verifying things before committing to them. 
The biggest opportunity I see is AI working as a kind of personalized tutor. ICS 314 moves fast and covers a lot, and having something that can explain in a way tailored to exactly where you're confused at any time of day is super valuable. 

## VI. Comparative Analysis
The thing AI can do that lectures and textbooks fundamentally can't is respond in the moment. When I was confused at midnight before something was due, and there's no professor or TA available, I had Google, I had overflow, and I had Claude. 
That said, the traditional structure of the course made that useful. The WODs, the deadlines, the hands-on problem solving created the pressure that made actually learning the material necessary. Without that structure, AI would have just been an easy way to get through assignments without understanding anything.

## VII. Future Considerations
AI is only going to play a bigger role in software engineering going forward, and that will change what it means to be a good developer. A lot of the routine implementation work is already being handed off to AI tools, so the skills that will matter more are things like understanding systems at a higher level, knowing how to debug generated code, and making good architectural decisions.

That means courses like ICS 314 should probably be intentional about protecting the experiences that build those foundational skills, but also teach students how to use AI responsibly: knowing how to write a good prompt, how to spot when it’s wrong, and when to push back.

## VIII. Conclusion
Looking back, my use of AI in ICS 314 was mostly supplementary. I used Claude for quality assurance, understanding others’ code, working through backend concepts, and debugging, but I kept it out of the WODs, essays, documentation, and anything else where doing the work myself was important for learning.

The stuff I did independently is what actually stuck, and the targeted AI use saved me real time without getting in the way of that. My advice to anyone going into a course like this would be to treat AI like a resource you can use when you need it, not as a default.
